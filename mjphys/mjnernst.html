Nernst equation

Nernst equation states the relation \begin{equation} E_{\rm cell} = E^0_{\rm cell} - 2.303 \frac{\displaystyle RT }{\displaystyle zF} \ln Q \end{equation} with cell potential $E_{\rm cell}$, standard cell reduction potential $ E^0_{\rm cell}$, universal gas constant $R = 8.314 ~ {\rm J}\cdot{K}^{-1}\cdot{\rm mol}^{-1}$, temperature in kelvin $T$, number of electrons transferred in the cell reaction $z$, Faraday constant $F = 9.649 \times 10^4 ~ {\rm C}\cdot{\rm mol}^{-1}$, and reaction quotient of the cell reaction $Q$. For a reaction \begin{equation} \alpha A + \beta B \leftrightharpoons \gamma D + \delta D, \end{equation} the reaction quotient is \begin{equation} Q = \frac{\displaystyle [C]^\gamma ~ [D]^\delta}{\displaystyle [A]^\alpha ~ [B]^\beta}. \end{equation} And standar cell potential is \begin{equation} E^0_{\rm cell} = E^0_{\rm cathode} - E^0_{\rm anode}. \end{equation} Reduction reaction occurs at cathode, while oxidation reaction occurs at anode.
